📖 How This Actually Works

🎲 What Even Is Monte Carlo?

Monte Carlo simulation is named after the famous casino in Monaco - because the core idea is to use random numbers to solve problems that would be too hard to solve mathematically. Instead of deriving an exact formula for "what will my revenue look like in 5 years?", you just… roll the dice. Thousands of times. Then look at what typically happened.

Imagine you want to know the probability of rain next month. Instead of solving the atmosphere's equations (impossible), you build a model that generates thousands of possible "next months" based on historical weather patterns, and count how many were rainy. That's Monte Carlo.

The casino analogy: You can't predict a single roulette spin, but if you simulate 100,000 spins you'll see the house edge emerge perfectly. Uncertainty in individual events becomes statistical certainty in aggregate.

📐 Step 1: What Statistics We Extract From Your Data

Before simulating anything, we compute the following from your selected rows and column:

Historical Statistics μ (mu) = mean of all values = Σxᵢ / n
σ (sigma) = standard deviation = √[ Σ(xᵢ−μ)² / (n−1) ]
μ_LR = mean log return = mean of ln(xᵢ / xᵢ₋₁)
σ_LR = std of log returns = std dev of ln(xᵢ / xᵢ₋₁)

Notice we use n−1 in the denominator for standard deviation - this is called Bessel's correction, and it gives an unbiased estimate of the true population variance from a sample.

Log returns (ln of the ratio between consecutive values) are preferred over simple returns in financial modeling because they are time-additive - you can sum log returns over periods, but you can't sum simple returns that way.

🔔 Step 2: Generating Random Numbers - The Box-Muller Transform

To simulate realistic random shocks we need numbers drawn from a normal (bell curve) distribution. Computers can only generate uniform random numbers (equally likely to be anything between 0 and 1). The Box-Muller transform converts two uniform numbers into one standard normal number:

Box-Muller Transform (1958) Given: U₁, U₂ ~ Uniform(0, 1)
Z = √(−2 ln U₁) × cos(2π U₂)

Result: Z ~ Normal(0, 1) ✓

This gives us a standard normal variable Z. We then scale it: to get a random number from Normal(μ, σ), we compute μ + σ·Z.

🧮 Step 3: The Four Simulation Methods

This simulator offers four different mathematical models. Each has a different assumption about how your data evolves over time:

📈
Normal / AR(1)
Assumes the next value depends linearly on the current value, plus random noise. Good for stationary data with mean reversion.
📊
Log-Normal
Values can't go negative. Models percentage growth. Industry standard for stock prices, revenue, and most financial series.
🔄
Bootstrap
No assumptions about distribution. Directly resamples your actual historical returns. Most honest when data is non-normal.
🌊
GBM
Geometric Brownian Motion - the model underlying Black-Scholes. Continuous compounding with constant drift and volatility.
AR(1) - Normal Distribution Method x_next = α + β × x_current + σ_resid × Z
where α, β are fitted by OLS regression of xᵢ on xᵢ₋₁
Log-Normal (Itô-corrected GBM) x_next = x × exp( (μ_LR − ½σ_LR²) + σ_LR × Z )

The −½σ² term is the Itô correction - without it you overestimate expected growth.
This comes from Itô's lemma in stochastic calculus.
GBM (Geometric Brownian Motion) x_next = x × exp( (μ_LR − ½σ_LR²) + σ_LR × Z )

The Itô correction ensures E[x_next] = x · exp(μ_LR) as intended -
matching the observed average growth rate in your data.

The Itô correction explained simply: If a stock grows 10% then falls 10%, you don't break even - you lose 1%. The −½σ² term corrects for this asymmetry in exponential compounding. Without it, your simulation thinks average returns compound linearly, which they don't. This is why E[e^X] ≠ e^E[X] when X is random.

📉 Step 4: Percentiles & What They Mean

After running, say, 10,000 simulations, we have 10,000 possible "next values". We sort them and read off percentiles using linear interpolation (the R-7 method):

Percentile Calculation For sorted array S of length n, percentile p:
index = (p / 100) × (n − 1)
result = S[floor(index)] + frac(index) × (S[ceil(index)] − S[floor(index)])

P10 means "10% of simulations ended up below this value" - a pessimistic scenario. P90 means "90% ended up below this" - an optimistic scenario. P50 is the median - the most likely outcome.

Don't confuse mean and median: In a skewed distribution (like many financial series), the mean can be pulled far right by a few explosive upside scenarios. The P50 (median) is usually the more honest "base case" - half the simulations landed above it, half below.

⚠️ Limitations & Honest Caveats

No model is perfect. Here's what you should know about this one:

1. Garbage in, garbage out. The simulator calibrates on your historical data. If your data has structural breaks - a pandemic, a merger, a market crash - the calibrated parameters may not reflect the future. The model can only learn from what it's been given.

2. Log-normal methods require positive values. Log-Normal and GBM methods compute growth rates between consecutive values. If your data contains zeros or negatives, those rows are excluded from the return calculation. Use the Normal (AR1) or Bootstrap methods for data that can go negative.

3. Stationarity is assumed. All four methods assume the statistical properties of your data - mean, variance, drift - stay roughly constant over time. In reality, volatility clusters and trends shift. If your data has obvious regime changes, narrow your row selection to the most relevant recent period.

4. Steps are independent. Each simulated step is drawn independently. Real-world financial and operational data often shows autocorrelation - good months tend to follow good months. This simulator doesn't model that clustering, which means tail risk may be slightly underestimated.

5. This is a planning tool, not a guarantee. No simulation can predict the future. Use the percentile range (P10–P90) as a thinking framework for optimistic vs. pessimistic scenarios - not as a precise forecast.
